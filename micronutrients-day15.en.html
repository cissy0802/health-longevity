Vitamin D, B12, Iron for Women, the Many Roles of Magnesium

Evidence base: mostly RCTs and authoritative consensus; doses synthesized from the Endocrine Society, WHO, and Attia/Patrick/Sims. One thread runs through all four — test before you supplement
CORE · Test First
Evidence: RCT (VITAL) + Endocrine Society consensus
Vitamin D: Measure Before You Supplement — More Isn't Better
Vitamin D — Test First, More Isn't Better
Bottom line
Vitamin D is a "fill-the-gap" nutrient, not a "more-is-stronger" one. Test serum 25(OH)D first, supplement only if low; target 30–50 ng/mL. Most people do fine on 1000–2000 IU/day, and megadosing people who aren't deficient adds no benefit and carries risk.
Science + mechanism
Vitamin D is actually a hormone precursor regulating calcium-phosphate metabolism, bone, immunity, and muscle. Skin makes it from UVB, but high latitude, indoor life, darker skin, sunscreen, and aging all blunt synthesis. Serum 25(OH)D is the gold-standard marker. Key evidence: the VITAL trial (Manson 2019, NEJM, n=25,871) gave the general population 2000 IU/day for 5 years and found no reduction in cancer incidence or cardiovascular events — confirming that supplementing people who aren't short does nothing. But correcting genuine deficiency does help bone density and fall prevention. Activation of vitamin D also requires magnesium as a cofactor (low magnesium impairs conversion), and pairing with vitamin K2 helps direct calcium into bone rather than arteries.

• Test: 25(OH)D, target 30–50 ng/mL.
• Dose: 1000–2000 IU/day to maintain; for severe deficiency, 4000–5000 IU/day short-term, retest at 3 months.
• Cofactors: pair with magnesium and K2 (90–120 mcg).
• Absorption: fat-soluble — take with a fat-containing meal.
• Don't megadose blindly: don't stay above 4000 IU long-term without retesting.
For women + myths
Adequate vitamin D in pregnancy is linked to lower risk of preeclampsia and low birth weight; test and correct during pregnancy. In perimenopause, declining estrogen plus low vitamin D/calcium accelerates bone loss — it's one line of the osteoporosis defense.
Myth 1: "Sunlight is enough" — winter UVB at high latitudes can't drive synthesis, and sunscreen blocks it.
Myth 2: "Vitamin D prevents everything" — VITAL showed no extra cancer/CV protection in the non-deficient.
Myth 3: Megadosing without testing — risks hypercalcemia.

SUB · Easily Missed
Evidence: cohort + expert consensus
Vitamin B12: The Silent Gap in Vegans and Older Adults
Vitamin B12 — The Silent Gap in Vegans and Older Adults
Bottom line
B12 deficiency is common and silent in vegans, those 50+, and long-term metformin/PPI users; serum B12 alone is insensitive — add MMA / homocysteine when needed; correct with oral 1000 mcg, no injections required.
Science + mechanism
B12 (cobalamin) is essential for DNA synthesis, red-cell production, and nerve myelin maintenance, and is found only in animal foods. Absorption depends on stomach acid and intrinsic factor, so atrophic gastritis, chronic PPIs, metformin, and GI surgery all impair it. Deficiency causes megaloblastic anemia and irreversible neurological damage (peripheral neuropathy, cognitive decline). The trap: neurological symptoms can precede anemia, and serum B12 is unreliable in the "gray zone" (200–400 pg/mL). Functional markers — methylmalonic acid (MMA) and homocysteine — rise earlier and reflect true tissue-level deficiency.

High-dose oral is enough for nearly all deficiencies — no injections needed (except severe neuro symptoms or major malabsorption). Methyl- and cyanocobalamin both work.
For women + myths
B12 needs rise in pregnancy and lactation; vegan mothers must supplement — maternal deficiency directly impairs fetal neurodevelopment, and breast-milk B12 depends on maternal status.
Myth 1: "Eating meat means you can't be deficient" — those with malabsorption (gastritis/PPI/post-surgery) still are.
Myth 2: "Normal serum B12 means you're fine" — gray-zone values need functional markers like MMA.
Myth 3: "Deficiency requires injections" — high-dose oral works just as well.

THIS WEEK
Check the risk list: are you or an older relative vegan / 50+ / on long-term metformin or acid blockers? If so, add B12 to the next blood draw.

CORE · Women First
Evidence: RCT (alternate-day iron) + WHO consensus
Iron & Women: Watch Ferritin, Not Just Hemoglobin
Iron & Women — Watch Ferritin, Not Just Hemoglobin
Bottom line
Women of reproductive age are the top-risk group for iron deficiency; normal hemoglobin ≠ adequate iron — what matters is ferritin; and a single alternate-day dose absorbs better with fewer side effects than daily split doses.
Science + mechanism
Iron drives hemoglobin oxygen transport, mitochondrial energy, and thyroid/neurotransmitter synthesis. Menstrual blood loss and pregnancy make women's iron needs far higher than men's. Deficiency develops in three stages: storage iron depletes first (ferritin falls) → red-cell production becomes limited → only then does anemia appear (Hb drops). So by the time Hb is abnormal it's already late; ferritin is the earliest, most sensitive signal of iron stores. Even without anemia, low ferritin causes fatigue, hair loss, poor exercise tolerance, and restless legs. Iron dosing raises liver hepcidin, which suppresses absorption for the next several hours — Stoffel 2017 (Lancet Haematol) showed alternate-day single doses give higher total absorption with fewer GI side effects.
Protocol
Ferritin strata (ng/mL) — below the green zone you can be symptomatic even without anemia
Depleted <15Stores empty; fatigue, hair loss
Low 15–30Borderline; supplement if symptomatic
Adequate >30Athletes/hair-loss target ~50
• Test: ferritin + transferrin saturation; prioritize for fatigued/hair-shedding women.
• Supplement: 60–100 mg elemental iron, once every other day, with vitamin C, away from calcium/coffee/tea.
• Retest: 8–12 weeks.
• Food: red meat and liver are rich in heme iron; pair plant iron with vitamin C.
For women + myths
Heavy periods (>80 ml per cycle, or frequent product changes) are the hidden driver of iron loss; recurrent deficiency warrants checking for fibroids and similar causes. Pregnancy sharply raises iron needs — monitored in routine prenatal care. The perimenopausal stretch of irregular, sometimes heavier bleeding is also a vulnerable window.
Myth 1: "Normal Hb means iron is fine" — stores are already empty; Hb is the last marker to fall.
Myth 2: "Daily dosing absorbs faster" — hepcidin makes multiple daily doses less efficient.
Myth 3: "Just megadose iron" — iron overload (men, post-menopausal women, hereditary hemochromatosis) is harmful; dose on test results.
This week + reflection
THIS WEEK
If you're often tired or shedding hair lately, add ferritin to your panel (many standard packages only test Hb and miss it). If already on iron, switch to alternate-day dosing with vitamin C.

SUB · Widely Deficient
Evidence: RCT + mechanistic
Magnesium: The Underrated Multitasker, Common Yet Hard to Measure
Magnesium — The Underrated Multitasker
Bottom line
Magnesium powers 300+ enzymatic reactions and is widely under-consumed, yet serum magnesium hides the truth (99% sits in cells and bone); prioritize food, and for supplements choose an absorbable form (glycinate/citrate), not oxide.
Science + mechanism
Magnesium is a cofactor for ATP energy metabolism, nerve and muscle conduction, glucose control, blood pressure, DNA synthesis, and vitamin D activation. The RDA is 310–320 mg for women and 400–420 mg for men, but processed food and soil depletion leave most people short. The measurement problem: serum magnesium is only ~1% of body stores and is tightly defended, so a normal serum value cannot rule out tissue deficiency (subclinical magnesium deficiency, DiNicolantonio 2018, Open Heart). Evidence-backed benefits of repletion: better sleep quality, modest blood-pressure lowering, improved insulin sensitivity, and relief of constipation and muscle cramps.

• Supplement: 200–400 mg elemental magnesium/day, taken before bed (aids sleep).
• Caution: those with impaired kidney function need medical guidance.
For women + myths
Magnesium can ease premenstrual symptoms (mood, bloating, cramps) and menstrual migraine (moderate evidence); in perimenopause it supports sleep and mood stability. Leg cramps in pregnancy are also often linked to low magnesium.
Myth 1: "Normal serum magnesium = not deficient" — serum doesn't reflect tissue stores.
Myth 2: "All magnesium is the same" — oxide is poorly absorbed and mostly acts as a laxative.
Myth 3: Treating magnesium as a cure-all — it corrects deficiency well, but offers little to those who aren't short.
This week + reflection
THIS WEEK
Read your magnesium supplement label — if it's oxide, switch to glycinate next time, take it before bed, and watch a week of sleep onset and quality. Or start from food: a handful of nuts plus a serving of leafy greens daily.

🔬 Reflections

① Does VITAL's null result mean supplementing vitamin D is useless?
No. VITAL enrolled a general population that was largely not deficient, so supplementing them naturally showed no extra benefit — which proves "supplementing the non-deficient does nothing," not "vitamin D is useless." For the truly deficient, repletion still has clear value for bone and fall prevention. This is a recurring lesson in nutrition: the effect of a gap-type nutrient appears only in deficient people, and a whole-population RCT dilutes it with the non-deficient. So individual decisions should follow your own test value, not the headline.
② Why does "test before you supplement" matter especially for micronutrients?
Because almost all micronutrients follow a U-shaped curve: deficiency harms, and excess harms too (vitamin D → hypercalcemia, iron overload → liver/heart damage, over-supplementing one can mask another). Unlike "eat more vegetables," which has essentially no ceiling, micronutrients mean dosing blind if you don't test. Testing turns "I feel deficient" into "I'm actually in this range," avoiding both wasted money and overdose. This is what Attia and Patrick keep stressing: measure first, then intervene.
③ The serum-marker trap: why can't B12, magnesium, and iron be judged on standard blood values alone?
All three can show "normal in blood, depleted in tissue." B12: serum is insensitive in the gray zone — you need functional markers like MMA/homocysteine. Magnesium: 99% lives in cells and bone, and serum is tightly defended, so a normal value masks subclinical deficiency. Iron: Hb is the last endpoint to fall, while ferritin captures early changes in stores. The shared lesson: choosing the right sensitive early marker matters far more than repeatedly checking the one that goes abnormal last.
④ What supplementation intuition does alternate-day iron overturn?
It overturns "the more you lack, the more often you should dose." Stoffel's work showed a single iron dose raises hepcidin and suppresses absorption for ~24 hours, so much of "multiple daily doses" is simply excreted while adding GI side effects. Alternate-day single dosing actually gives higher total uptake. The reminder: the body has feedback control, so more input ≠ more absorption; for many supplements the optimum is "work with physiology," not "pile on volume."
⑤ Compress all four micronutrients into a one-sentence prescription.
Test first, supplement only the gap, dose to just-right without overshooting. Vitamin D: test 25(OH)D, top up to 30–50 ng/mL; B12: screen vegans and older adults, oral 1000 mcg if low; iron: watch ferritin, women first, alternate-day with vitamin C; magnesium: food first, glycinate before bed. All four share one philosophy — this is "filling a gap," not "more is stronger."
