EMERGENCY · Neuro / Time Window
Stroke: BE-FAST and "Time Is Brain"
Recognizing Stroke Fast Enough to Treat It
Bottom Line
Sudden facial droop, limb weakness, or slurred speech — even if it resolves in minutes — means call an ambulance now. In ischemic stroke, every minute of delay kills about 1.9 million neurons.
Evidence Grade
Guideline + RCT: AHA/ASA 2019 acute ischemic stroke guidelines. IV thrombolysis window is 4.5 h (NINDS 1995); endovascular thrombectomy extends to 24 h in selected cases (DAWN, DEFUSE-3). "1.9 million neurons per minute" is from Saver 2006, Stroke.
Science & Mechanism
The brain is exquisitely sensitive to oxygen loss. Once a vessel is blocked (ischemic, ~85%) or ruptures (hemorrhagic), downstream tissue dies by the minute. Thrombolysis and thrombectomy restore flow, but only work early — once tissue has infarcted, reperfusion adds bleeding risk. Stroke is a "watch the clock" disease, not a "how bad does it feel" one — a TIA (mini-stroke) resolves on its own yet is the strongest warning of a major stroke.

Any positive sign → call emergency services and say "suspected stroke" (route to a stroke center if possible). Onset time decides whether thrombolysis is possible, so record it precisely; if noticed on waking, use the "last known well" time. While waiting, lie the person flat with the head turned to prevent aspiration; give no food, water, or medication (surgery may be needed, swallowing may be impaired).
For Women + Common Myths
For women: oral contraceptives combined with "migraine with aura," plus pregnancy and the postpartum period, all raise stroke risk; atrial fibrillation carries higher stroke risk in women and warrants proper anticoagulation.
Myths: ① "Pinching the philtrum, bloodletting, pricking fingertips" wastes time, does nothing, and may worsen a hemorrhagic stroke; ② symptoms resolving ≠ all clear — the 48 h after a TIA is the peak for recurrence, so still go to the ER.

EMERGENCY · Cardiovascular
Heart Attack & Chest Pain: Don't Wait, Don't Drive Yourself
Acute Coronary Syndrome — Every Minute Is Muscle
Bottom Line
Crushing chest pain/pressure lasting more than a few minutes, with cold sweat, breathlessness, or pain radiating to the arm or jaw — treat it as a heart attack: call an ambulance and, absent contraindications, chew 300 mg of aspirin.
Evidence Grade
Guideline: AHA/ESC acute coronary syndrome guidelines. Reperfusion "door-to-balloon" target is <90 min — the sooner the vessel opens, the less muscle dies ("time is muscle"). Atypical presentation in women: AHA scientific statement (Mehta 2016, Circulation).
Science & Mechanism
A coronary plaque ruptures, a clot forms and blocks blood supply, and heart muscle infarcts. The damage is irreversible, expands over time, and can trigger fatal ventricular fibrillation. The core of first aid is fast reperfusion (emergency PCI or thrombolysis). Chewed aspirin inhibits platelets and slows clot growth — chewing absorbs faster than swallowing.
Actionable Protocol
Typical: retrosternal squeezing/tightness >5–10 min, not relieved by rest, radiating to the left arm/jaw/back, with cold sweat, nausea, a sense of doom.
① Call an ambulance now; ② stop activity, sit/half-lie down; ③ absent bleeding or allergy contraindications, chew 300 mg aspirin; ④ unlock the door, ready the medical history and medication list.
Do not drive yourself (you may fibrillate en route). Nitroglycerin only if you have diagnosed angina and blood pressure is not low.
For Women + Common Myths
For women: women, people with diabetes, and older adults more often present atypically — dominated by profound fatigue, breathlessness, nausea, upper-abdominal/back pain, easily mistaken for "indigestion" or "tiredness" and thus delayed. Young and peripartum women should be alert to SCAD (spontaneous coronary artery dissection).
Myth: a heart attack always means severe pain — "silent MI" is common in diabetics and the elderly; toughing it out or taking a quick heart remedy and "waiting to see" is the most dangerous delay.

EMERGENCY · Infection / Allergy · Minute-Level
Sepsis & Anaphylaxis: Two Minute-Level Emergencies
When Infection or Allergy Turns Into Shock
Bottom Line
Sepsis is whole-body organ failure driven by infection; anaphylaxis is circulatory/airway collapse after an allergen. Both run on hours-to-minutes, and every step of delayed recognition sharply raises mortality.
Evidence Grade
Consensus: Sepsis-3 (Singer 2016, JAMA) defines it as "infection + organ dysfunction"; each hour of delayed antibiotics raises mortality (Kumar 2006, Crit Care Med). Anaphylaxis: EAACI/WAO guidelines — first line is intramuscular epinephrine; antihistamines and steroids are not rescue drugs.
Science & Mechanism
Sepsis: an out-of-control immune response to infection damages the body's own vessels and organs, producing low blood pressure, altered consciousness, and low urine output. Anaphylaxis: IgE-mediated mast-cell degranulation causes vasodilation, airway edema, and bronchospasm — lethal within minutes. Epinephrine constricts vessels, dilates airways, and stabilizes mast cells; it is the only drug that reverses it.
Actionable Protocol
Sepsis red flags (infection + any of these → ER): confusion/drowsiness, rapid breathing (≥22/min), rigors with high fever or hypothermia, mottled or clammy skin, sharply reduced urine, systolic BP ≤100 mmHg.
Anaphylaxis (within minutes of food/drug/sting): whole-body hives/swelling + difficulty breathing/throat tightness/stridor, or dizziness/loss of consciousness → inject epinephrine 0.3–0.5 mg IM into the mid outer thigh immediately, repeat in 5–15 min if needed; call an ambulance; lie flat with legs raised (half-sitting if breathing is hard). Seek care even if it eases (biphasic reaction).

For Women + Common Myths
For women: if anaphylaxis occurs in pregnancy, give epinephrine anyway — do not hesitate because of pregnancy; delay harms mother and fetus more. Postpartum endometritis and urinary tract infection are common sources of puerperal sepsis.
Myths: ① taking an antihistamine first to "tide over" an allergy — delaying epinephrine is the leading cause of anaphylaxis deaths; ② a fever is just a cold — early sepsis can also look like just "fever + unusually listless + breathless."

SKILL · Basic Life Support / Medication Safety
CPR, AED, Heimlich & the Home Medicine Cabinet
The Highest-Value Skills a Household Can Learn
Bottom Line
Surviving cardiac arrest hinges on whether a bystander starts compressions immediately and defibrillates early; knowing the AED, the Heimlich, and how to run a safe medicine cabinet are a family's best-value lifesaving skills.
Evidence Grade
Guideline: AHA 2020 CPR guidelines. Each minute of delayed defibrillation cuts survival 7–10% (Valenzuela 2000, NEJM); for adult non-asphyxial arrest, bystander "hands-only CPR" is equally effective (AHA).
Science & Mechanism
After arrest, blood flow stops and the brain begins irreversible injury in 4–6 min. Chest compressions substitute for the pump and maintain brain and heart perfusion; the AED corrects VF/VT. Most adult arrests stem from arrhythmia, so "compressions only" is enough to save a life; children, drowning, and choking need compressions + rescue breaths.
Actionable Protocol
CPR: unresponsive to a shoulder tap + not breathing normally → call for help, fetch an AED, start compressions: heel of the hand on the center of the chest (nipple line), rate 100–120/min, depth 5–6 cm, full recoil, minimal interruptions.
AED: power on, follow the voice prompts to place the pads, don't touch the person during analysis, deliver the shock when told, then resume compressions immediately.
Heimlich: choking, can't speak or cough → stand behind, fist above the navel, quick inward-and-upward thrusts; use chest thrusts for pregnant/obese people; alone, self-thrust over a chair back.

For Women + Common Myths
For women: acetaminophen is the preferred analgesic in pregnancy; avoid NSAIDs in late pregnancy (they affect the fetal ductus arteriosus). When performing CPR, keep the hands on the lower half of the sternum — don't let body shape mislead placement.
Myths: ① fear of cracking ribs or doing it wrong stops people from acting — the survival rate of doing nothing is 0, and ribs heal; ② "pinching the philtrum" is not resuscitation; ③ "IV drips cure faster" and "anti-inflammatories = antibiotics" are the most common medication errors.

Going Deeper
Why does first aid insist on "call an ambulance" over "drive there yourself"?
An ambulance moves the ER to the scene: en route it can monitor rhythm, defibrillate, and give drugs, and it can pre-alert the hospital to activate the cath lab or stroke pathway so treatment starts on arrival. Driving yourself means no one can help if you fibrillate or lose consciousness — and you lose that golden window.
What trade-off does the "time window" imply for stroke and heart attack?
The benefit of early reperfusion (thrombolysis/thrombectomy/PCI) decays sharply with time, but it also carries bleeding risk that needs imaging and expert judgment to weigh. That's exactly why you record the onset time and get to care fast for a clinician to decide — rather than agonizing at home over whether to go.
Why is epinephrine the only first-line drug for anaphylaxis, and not antihistamines/steroids?
It comes down to mechanism and speed: epinephrine constricts vessels, dilates the airway, and stabilizes mast cells within minutes, directly reversing the lethal steps. Antihistamines only ease itching and hives; steroids help prevent a delayed biphasic reaction hours later — neither rescues acute airway edema or circulatory collapse.
Why did bystander CPR simplify from "compress + breathe" to "compressions only"?
Simplifying lowers the barrier so more people will act. For adult cardiac arrest, residual blood oxygen plus uninterrupted high-quality compressions is enough early on; hesitation over mouth-to-mouth only interrupts compressions. The higher rate of people stepping in saves more lives overall.
What should a home cabinet guard against most — not "missing a drug," but what?
Duplication and interactions: stacking multiple combination cold remedies overdoses acetaminophen and harms the liver; warfarin + NSAIDs or certain herbal remedies cause bleeding. For most families, "take more, mix them, stockpile antibiotics" is far more dangerous than being short one medicine.
