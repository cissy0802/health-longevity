SUB · Circadian Rhythm / Light Entrainment
Timezone Adaptation: Light Is the Master Control, Direction Sets the Difficulty
Timezone Adaptation — Light Is the Master Clock
Bottom Line
Jet lag is really a mismatch between your internal clock and destination time. The body clock shifts only about 1 hour per day on its own, and flying east (phase advance) is harder than flying west (phase delay). Light is the strongest time cue — getting light at the right time and avoiding it at the wrong time beats any drug.
Evidence Level
Mechanism (human): Khalsa 2003 (J Physiol) mapped the human light phase-response curve (PRC) — light near the end of your biological night advances the clock; light just after biological dusk delays it. Cochrane meta: Herxheimer & Petrie 2002 pooled 10 RCTs — melatonin significantly reduces jet lag across ≥5 timezones, especially eastward (NNT ≈ 2). The clock's free-running period runs slightly longer than 24h, so "stretching the day" westward is easier.
Science + Mechanism
The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) is the master clock, calibrated by blue light via retinal melanopsin cells. After crossing timezones the SCN is out of sync with local light/dark, and peripheral clocks in the liver and gut realign even more slowly — producing internal desynchrony, the real source of daytime drowsiness, 3 a.m. wakefulness and gut upset. Key corollary: the timing of light decides which way the clock moves. Flying east you need to advance the clock → morning light; getting light at the wrong time (e.g. staying up under bright light after an eastward trip) pushes the clock the wrong way.

Women's Note + Common Myths
Melatonin may influence reproductive hormone signaling, so consult a doctor before use if trying to conceive, pregnant or breastfeeding. It is not a contraceptive, but repeatedly disrupting circadian rhythm with timezone changes can itself perturb the menstrual cycle (irregular cycles are more common in flight crew). On long-haul days, avoid stacking sleep drugs with alcohol.
Myth 1: melatonin is a sleeping pill — it is a timing signal; taken at the wrong hour it pushes the clock the wrong way.
Myth 2: force sleep on the plane regardless — sleep according to destination time, not raw tiredness.

SUB · Prolonged Sitting in Flight / Clots & Metabolism
Sitting Still at Altitude: Clot Risk & Metabolic Cost
Sitting Still at Altitude — Clot Risk & Metabolic Cost
Bottom Line
The real health risk of long-haul flight is not "radiation" but the stacked effect of immobility + mild hypoxia + dehydration on venous clots (VTE). Beyond 4 hours, risk rises roughly 2–3×, scaling with duration. Absolute risk stays low, and moving, hydrating and skipping alcohol cut it sharply.
Evidence Level
Large project + meta: the WHO WRIGHT study (2007) confirmed flights >4h roughly double VTE risk with a dose-response (longer = higher). Chandra 2009 (Ann Intern Med) meta: each additional 2h raises risk ~18%; symptomatic VTE after >4h is about 1/4600. Mechanism: cabin pressure of 0.75–0.81 atm gives oxygen equivalent to 1800–2400 m altitude — mild hypoxia plus hemoconcentration is pro-coagulant.
Science + Mechanism
Virchow's triad is fully present in flight: stasis (a bent knee compresses the popliteal vein during long sitting), hypercoagulability (hypoxia + dehydration concentrating the blood), and in some people endothelial/thrombophilic tendency. Sitting for hours also drops muscle lipoprotein-lipase activity, worsening post-meal glucose and triglyceride clearance — the metabolic side of feeling puffy and sluggish on arrival. Every calf-muscle contraction pumps venous blood back, which is why "moving" is the cheapest intervention.
Actionable Protocol
• Move: stand and walk every 1–2 hours; even seated, do ankle pumps (flex–point) 20–30 times per hour
• Hydrate: about 250 ml water per hour; avoid alcohol and excess caffeine (diuretic effect worsens dehydration and disrupts sleep)
• Seat choice: prefer the aisle on long-haul so getting up is easy
• Higher-risk people (recent surgery/fracture, cancer, pregnancy or postpartum, prior VTE, estrogen-containing contraception, marked obesity): wear 15–30 mmHg graduated compression stockings, and ask a doctor before travel whether low-molecular-weight heparin is warranted
• Do not take routine aspirin to prevent flight clots — evidence is weak and it is not the guideline first choice
Women's Note + Common Myths
Estrogen-containing oral contraceptives / hormone therapy raise baseline VTE risk, which matters more when combined with long-haul flight — high-risk users should prioritize compression stockings, movement and generous hydration, and can discuss it with a doctor beforehand. Pregnancy and the first 6 postpartum weeks are naturally hypercoagulable, so seek medical review before long-haul travel.
Myth 1: only older people get flight clots — younger people with thrombophilic factors can too.
Myth 2: aspirin is enough — graduated stockings + movement + hydration are the evidence-based combo.

SUB · Travel Nutrition / Meal-Timing Cues
Travel Nutrition: Meal Timing Is a "Clock Knob" Too
Travel Nutrition — Meal Timing as a Clock Cue
Bottom Line
Beyond light, meal timing is the second time-cue for peripheral organs (liver, gut). After landing, eat on the destination's meal schedule to help peripheral clocks realign — while guarding hydration and food hygiene so "traveler's diarrhea" doesn't wreck the trip.
Evidence Level
Mechanism (animal + emerging human): Fuller 2009 (Science, mice) showed time-restricted feeding entrains peripheral clocks like the liver, independent of light; human data suggest scheduled eating aids jet-lag recovery, though weaker than light. Traveler's diarrhea: Steffen 2015 (JAMA) review — 20–60% incidence in high-risk regions, mostly enterotoxigenic E. coli; CDC emphasizes food hygiene first, antibiotics for treatment when needed. The classic "Argonne anti-jet-lag diet" has weak evidence and is not strongly recommended.
Science + Mechanism
The SCN master clock is set by light, but peripheral clocks in liver, gut and pancreas are more sensitive to feed–fast signals. Keep eating on your origin schedule after crossing timezones and those clocks stay misaligned with the master clock and the destination, worsening gut upset and metabolic sluggishness. So "eat locally as soon as you land" is the lever that pulls peripheral clocks into line. A second thread is infection: unfamiliar microbes and contaminated food/water cause traveler's diarrhea, and the resulting dehydration in turn amplifies jet lag and clot risk.
Actionable Protocol
• Align meal times: from landing day, take all three meals on destination time; nudge the in-flight meal toward that rhythm too
• Optional light fast: a fast of ~12–16 h before landing, breaking it with the first local meal, can help "reboot" peripheral clocks (moderate evidence; use caution if you have a sensitive gut or diabetes)
• Food hygiene (medium/high-risk regions): "boil it, cook it, peel it" — drink only bottled/boiled water, don't brush teeth with tap water, avoid raw/cold food and pre-cut street fruit; treat ice with suspicion
• Hydration first: plenty of water + electrolytes in flight and hot climates; limit alcohol
• Diarrhea response: mild — oral rehydration + loperamide; fever or bloody stool — seek care; high-risk travel can carry azithromycin (per doctor). Probiotic prevention has weak evidence and is not strongly recommended
Women's Note + Common Myths
Crossing timezones plus disrupted eating can cause a brief menstrual-cycle shift in women — common and usually self-limiting. Pregnancy travel especially demands food and water hygiene (higher Listeria and Salmonella risk); avoid all raw, undercooked and unpasteurized dairy, and confirm any anti-diarrheal or antibiotic with an obstetrician.
Myth 1: "drink more coffee to stay sharp" in flight — caffeine is diuretic and sleep-disrupting, undoing hydration and timing efforts.
Myth 2: probiotics prevent traveler's diarrhea — evidence is weak; food hygiene is the main line.

SUB · Jet-Lag Sleep / Drug Strategy
Managing Jet-Lag Sleep: The Light, Melatonin, Caffeine & Nap Combo
Managing Jet-Lag Sleep — Light, Melatonin, Caffeine, Naps
Bottom Line
You can't brute-force sleep through jet lag — you orchestrate it: use light + caffeine to stay awake by day, low-dose melatonin + sleep hygiene to fall asleep at night, and keep naps <30 minutes. The goal is to move the sleep window onto destination night as fast as possible.
Evidence Level
Cochrane (melatonin, Herxheimer 2002): effective for jet-lag sleep, good safety. RCT: Beaumont 2004 (J Appl Physiol) showed slow-release caffeine markedly improved daytime alertness after eastward jet lag (at the cost of slightly disturbed first-night sleep). Consensus (AASM): short-acting hypnotics (e.g. zolpidem) can briefly aid night sleep but are not first choice and shouldn't be combined with alcohol. Nap science: <30 min refreshes without entering deep sleep, avoiding post-nap grogginess.
Science + Mechanism
Two drivers of jet-lag drowsiness: phase misalignment (forced awake during biological night) and accumulated sleep pressure (adenosine). Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors and temporarily suppresses drowsiness, but with a half-life of ~5–6 h, afternoon intake erodes night sleep. Melatonin given at biological "evening" advances phase and shortens sleep latency. A nap discharges some sleep pressure; too long and you drop into deep sleep, producing sleep inertia and stealing nighttime sleepiness — hence the "<30 min" cap.
Actionable Protocol
• Go local immediately: however tired by day, nap once, ≤20–30 min; sleep for real only at night
• Caffeine: only in the destination morning–midday; stop 8–10 h before target bedtime
• Melatonin: 0.5–3 mg before destination bedtime for 2–4 nights to aid phase alignment (not long-term daily use)
• Sleep hygiene: avoid blue/bright light before bed; keep the room dark, cool, quiet; use an eye mask and earplugs; on the plane, simulate night (eye mask + no screens) if you want to sleep
• Hypnotics: only occasional, short-acting, per doctor; never with alcohol, and cautious mid-flight (sitting + sedation raises clot risk)
Women's Note + Common Myths
Perimenopause already brings hot flashes and easy waking, and crossing timezones fragments sleep further — so nail cool, dark, low-caffeine conditions, with low-dose short-term melatonin as an aid. In pregnancy, favor non-drug measures (light, schedule, sleep hygiene); ask a doctor before any sleep aid or melatonin.
Myth 1: use alcohol to sleep — it shortens sleep onset but wrecks late-night REM and worsens dehydration and snoring; net effect is worse.
Myth 2: catch up with long daytime sleep — extended daytime sleep delays nighttime sleepiness and prolongs jet lag.

Deeper Questions
① Why is flying east harder to recover from than flying west?
The human clock's free-running period runs slightly longer than 24 hours, so it naturally "wants to stretch the day." Flying west requires exactly that — lengthening the day (phase delay), with the grain, easier. Flying east requires advancing the clock (phase advance), against the grain, harder. So for the same 6-timezone hop, eastward usually hurts more and recovers slower — the protocol should be more aggressive eastward: morning light, evening light avoidance, melatonin before bed.
② Why does the "timing" of light matter more than its brightness?
Because light's effect on the clock is governed by the phase-response curve (PRC): the same bright light near the end of biological night → advances the clock; just after biological dusk → delays it. Get the direction wrong and stronger light pushes the clock further the wrong way. So step one of jet-lag management isn't "get more sun," it's working out whether to seek or avoid light right now — the core algorithm of apps like Timeshifter.
③ For a short trip (returning in 1–2 days), should you shift for the timezone at all?
Often no. If the stay is very short, rather than shifting to destination time and then shifting back (two bouts of jet lag), it's usually better to keep your origin schedule: put key meetings in your "subjective daytime" and use light and caffeine tactically to stay alert. Also worth watching: chronic circadian disruption (years of timezone flying, long-term shift work) is epidemiologically linked to metabolic syndrome and mood disorders, so treating regular light, meals and sleep as portable "rhythm hygiene" is the right move from a longevity lens.
