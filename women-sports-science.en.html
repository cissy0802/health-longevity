Women Are Not Small Men
Stacy Sims' Female Sports Science · 女性运动科学

Special · based on Stacy Sims' ROAR & Next Level · BigCat's Vitality Protocol
This issue's stance: most mainstream training and nutrition advice comes from male subjects and doesn't always transfer to women. Stacy Sims' slogan "women are not small men" offers a useful sex-specific lens: the menstrual cycle, energy availability, and perimenopause all change what's optimal. But separate what's evidence-backed from what's her mechanistic inference—this issue flags both.
THESIS · SEX DIFFERENCES
Evidence: mechanistic / expert opinion
"Women are not small men": why generic advice often fails
Women Are Not Small Men
Bottom line
Much of sports science is built on male subjects, then "scaled by body weight" onto women. Sims argues women differ systematically in hormones, metabolism, thermoregulation, and recovery—so shrinking a male plan is often not optimal, and sometimes counterproductive.
Science + mechanism
Cyclic estrogen/progesterone shifts affect substrate use (relatively more fat oxidation), thermoregulation (higher core temp in the luteal phase), fluid and sodium balance, and central fatigue; muscle-fiber makeup and heat dissipation differ too. Historically women were excluded from exercise research (on the excuse that the cycle "confounds data"), leaving the evidence base male-skewed—a real scientific gap. But note: acknowledging difference ≠ every women-specific prescription is RCT-proven.
Actionable protocol
• Make the "female lens" the default: don't copy male influencers'/textbooks' fasted, low-carb, cardio-only playbook
• Fix the three things women most often under-do: eat enough (energy availability), lift heavy (resistance), and cover iron / vitamin D
• Self-regulate by symptoms and performance, not by forcing a rigid "cycle formula"
Key points + myths
This is not "women should train lighter and less"—the opposite. Women have long been told to do "light weights, high reps, lots of cardio," yet the evidence supports that women also need heavy loads, strength, and high intensity.
Myths: ① "women's training = light-weight toning"—wrong; women adapt well to (and need) resistance and strength; ② "just scale a male plan by body weight"—ignores hormonal and metabolic differences.
Key references
• Sims ST, Yeager S. ROAR. 2016.
• Costello JT, et al. Where are all the female participants in sports and exercise research? Eur J Sport Sci. 2014.

CYCLE · PERIODIZATION
Evidence: mechanism fair / cycle-periodization RCTs weak
Training with your cycle: a useful frame, not an iron law
Training & Fueling Across the Menstrual Cycle
Bottom line
In the follicular phase (first half), estrogen dominates and you can usually push intensity more; in the luteal phase (second half), progesterone rises, so core temp is higher, central fatigue greater, protein/carb/sodium needs rise, and sleep may suffer. Nudging training and fueling accordingly is a reasonable frame—not an iron law.
Science + mechanism
The high-hormone luteal state adds thermoregulatory load and slightly more muscle breakdown, on top of menstrual iron loss. Sims suggests more protein and carbs, sodium/fluids, and dialing back intensity if needed in the luteal phase. But here's the catch: RCT evidence for strict "cycle-based periodization" is currently weak and inconsistent (McNulty 2020's review found only a trivial overall effect of cycle phase on performance, on low-quality evidence)—individual variation is huge, and combined oral contraceptives change the hormonal picture entirely. So symptom-driven autoregulation beats rigid formulas.
Actionable protocol
• Feeling flat (often menses/luteal)? reduce volume rather than grind; feeling good (often follicular)? schedule heavy lifts/sprints
• Luteal: more protein (30–40g/meal), carbs around hard sessions, mind hydration and sodium
• Menses: prioritize iron (especially heavy flow); if performance dips for no reason, check ferritin
• Log "energy/sleep/performance" over 2–3 cycles to learn your own pattern
Key points + myths
Women on combined oral contraceptives don't have natural hormonal swings—don't force a "follicular/luteal" split; go by feel and performance.
Myths: ① "no exercise during your period"—for most, gentle exercise actually eases symptoms; ② "the cycle app says luteal = light only"—too absolute; many train fine in the luteal phase, judge by the day.
Key references
• Sims ST, Yeager S. ROAR. 2016.
• McNulty KL, et al. The effects of menstrual cycle phase on exercise performance: a systematic review and meta-analysis. Sports Med. 2020.

FUEL · ENERGY AVAILABILITY
Evidence: LEA/REDs evidence strong
Women fear "under-fueling" more: the fasted & fasting trap
Fuel Up — Fasted Training, IF & Low Energy Availability
Bottom line
Sims stresses that active women are especially sensitive to low energy availability (LEA). Chronic "fasted morning training + intermittent fasting + low-carb" can suppress hormones, cost muscle, harm bone, and disrupt menstruation—i.e., REDs (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport).
Science + mechanism
When intake chronically lags expenditure, the body down-regulates "non-essential" functions: sex hormones, thyroid, bone formation, immunity. Women's hypothalamic–pituitary axis is more sensitive to an energy gap, which can produce functional hypothalamic amenorrhea and bone loss. This clashes directly with the fasted/fasting/very-low-carb trends many women embrace. REDs is an internationally recognized syndrome (IOC/BJSM), so this recommendation rests on relatively firm evidence.
Actionable protocol
• Don't train fasted chronically, especially in the morning: eat something pre-session (banana/toast/whey)
• On hard days, fuel carbs around training; don't stack low-carb or fasting on high training loads
• Get enough protein: active women ~1.6–2.2 g/kg, 30–40g post-training
• Watch for REDs signs: missed/irregular periods, persistent fatigue, frequent injury/illness, stress fractures
Key points + myths
Fat loss shouldn't come from starving either. Very low energy costs muscle, lowers metabolism, and disrupts cycles; enough protein + resistance + a modest deficit is what's sustainable (same logic as our "Evidence-Based Weight Loss" issue).
Myths: ① "fasted cardio burns more fat"—a short-term substrate difference isn't a long-term fat-loss edge, and it carries a hormonal cost for women; ② "intermittent fasting suits everyone"—active women and the already energy-strapped tend to be harmed most.
Key references
• Mountjoy M, et al. 2023 IOC consensus statement on REDs. Br J Sports Med. 2023.
• Sims ST, Yeager S. ROAR. 2016.

MENOPAUSE · Next Level
Evidence: resistance/protein/creatine strong; exact dosing partly her synthesis
Change tactics for perimenopause: lift heavy, move fast, eat enough protein
Perimenopause & Menopause — Next Level
Bottom line
Falling estrogen reshapes muscle, bone, body composition, and recovery. In Next Level, Sims argues you should ramp intensity up, not down: heavy resistance + power/plyometrics (for bone) + sprint intervals + ample protein + creatine, rather than ever-more moderate jogging.
Science + mechanism
Less estrogen → a blunted muscle-building signal, faster bone loss, easier visceral-fat gain, slower recovery. The counter-logic: use stronger mechanical stimulus (heavy loads, impact) against sarcopenia and osteoporosis; use higher protein to overcome "anabolic resistance"; creatine has good evidence for muscle and cognition in menopausal women. Cut aimless long moderate cardio (poor payoff, can raise cortisol). Resistance + protein + creatine are on firm ground; the exact "sprints + jumps" dosing is more her mechanistic synthesis.
Actionable protocol
• Strength: heavy compound lifts (squat/deadlift/press/pull), lower reps, progressive overload
• Bone: jumping/landing/impact moves (no joint contraindication) + loaded training
• High intensity: short sprint intervals (SIT) over lots of slow cardio
• Protein: ~1.6–2.2 g/kg, 30–40g post-training/before bed; creatine 3–5 g/day
• Recovery: sleep and protein; don't let training pile into chronic fatigue
Key points + myths
This aligns with our "Perimenopause & Hormones" issue: menopausal hormone therapy (MHT) is a separate discussion with your doctor; the exercise piece stands on its own and is powerful—lift heavy, don't just do yoga and walks.
Myths: ① "get older, train lighter, rest more"—you actually need heavier and more explosive work to fight sarcopenia and bone loss; ② "creatine is a male/bodybuilder thing"—it benefits women's muscle and brain (especially in perimenopause) and is safe.
Key references
• Sims ST, Yeager S. Next Level. 2022.
• Smith-Ryan AE, et al. Creatine supplementation in women's health: a lifespan perspective. Nutrients. 2021.

🔬 Reflections — 深入思考

① Why has sports science defaulted to male subjects for so long?
Women were historically excluded on the grounds that "the cycle confounds data" and "recruitment is easier," leaving many guidelines built on male physiology. This is not just a fairness problem but an accuracy one—it's why women-specific advice still lacks high-quality evidence. Closing that research gap is the precondition for "evidence-based" to actually mean what it says.
② Which of Sims' recommendations should you follow, and which should you watch?
On firm ground (do them): avoid low energy availability/REDs, eat enough protein, lift heavy + take creatine in perimenopause, be cautious with chronic fasting as an active woman. Still mechanistic or thinly evidenced: strict menstrual-cycle "periodization" formulas and some phase-precise nutrient doses. The safe strategy: take the former as your foundation, treat the latter as testable, individual hypotheses.
③ "Cycle syncing" went mainstream—empowerment or a new cage?
Both. It got women to take a long-ignored physiological rhythm seriously—progress; but packaged into apps and paid courses, it easily turns absolute ("no training in the luteal phase"), manufacturing new anxiety and limits. The best use is as a "listen to your body" prompt, not another set of rules to obey.
④ Why does "eat less + more cardio" so often backfire for women who want to improve?
Because it hits two things women are more sensitive to at once: an energy deficit, and the cortisol load of chronic cardio. The result is lost muscle, a lower metabolism, disrupted cycles, and mounting fatigue. Sims (and our "Weight Loss" issue) point the opposite way: eat enough protein, lift heavy, use high intensity smartly—"less is more" doesn't hold here.
