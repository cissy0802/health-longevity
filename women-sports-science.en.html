1Women are not small men

A lot of the "efficient" plans you scroll past—fasted runs, keto, cardio only, light-weight/high-rep—are based on male research or built for men. Women differ in hormones, metabolism, thermoregulation, and recovery, so copying them often does nothing, or disrupts your cycle and costs you muscle.

The good news: the three things that actually help women are the ones most often skipped—eat enough, lift heavy, and cover iron and vitamin D.

Evidence: direction is clear; specific prescriptions still accruing

✅ Do this

  • Strength first: 2–3 resistance sessions/week, built on big compound moves (squat, deadlift, press, pull, row).
  • Make the weight count: pick a load that's clearly hard by 6–12 reps, not a token light dumbbell. Women's low testosterone means lifting builds tone, not bulk.
  • Cover the two most-missed nutrients: iron (especially with heavy periods) and vitamin D. Not sure? Get bloods once—ferritin and 25(OH)D.
  • Drop the "male defaults": fasted morning training, casual fasting, cardio-only—replaced one by one below.

2Training with your cycle—as a frame, not a law

Rough pattern: from your period to ovulation (first half) you usually have energy and can push hard; after ovulation to your next period (second half—the week or so before your period), hormones rise and you may run hotter, tire more easily, sleep worse, and crave carbs. You can nudge training accordingly—but variation is huge, so don't treat an app's "cycle formula" as an order.

Evidence: affects how you feel (mechanistic); strict cycle periodization for performance is weak

✅ Do this

  • Track first, adjust second: spend 10 seconds a day noting energy / sleep / how training felt + which day of your cycle. In 2–3 months your own pattern beats any generic formula.
  • Go by state, not the calendar: feeling good → schedule heavy lifts or sprints; feeling flat → drop to easy cardio or skill work, don't grind.
  • Three small things in the pre-period week: +5–10g protein per meal; carbs around hard sessions; more water and a little extra sodium (a pinch of salt/electrolytes when sweaty). Sleeping badly? Get to bed earlier.
  • Heavy periods → prioritize iron: red meat / liver / legumes with vitamin C to absorb; if performance dips for no reason, check ferritin.
Note: if you're on combined oral contraceptives you don't have natural hormonal swings—skip the "follicular/luteal" split and just go by feel.

3Fuel first—the fasted & fasting trap

This is the point Sims stresses most, and it has the firmest evidence: once an active woman chronically eats far less than she burns, the body shuts down "non-essential" functions—sacrificing menstruation, bone, thyroid, immunity first. The combo of fasted morning training + intermittent fasting + low-carb is the easiest way to get burned (clinically: REDs, relative energy deficiency in sport).

Evidence: strong (REDs is an internationally recognized syndrome)

✅ Do this

  • Don't train fasted: 30–60 min before, eat easy-to-digest carbs + a little protein. E.g. half a banana + a scoop of whey; or toast + yogurt; too rushed at dawn? a few sips of honey water / an energy gel.
  • Remember this protein number: 1.6–2.2 g per kg of body weight per day. ~60 kg = 100–130 g, split over 3–4 meals at 30–40 g each.
  • Add carbs on hard days: rice/noodles/potato/fruit, around training; don't stack low-carb or fasting on high-volume days.
  • Refuel after: within 30–60 min, a meal or snack with 30–40 g protein + carbs.
What 30 g of protein looks like: a palm of chicken/fish/lean meat; or a 150 g pot of Greek yogurt + 2 eggs; or a scoop of whey + a glass of milk; or a ~200 g block of firm tofu + a handful of edamame.

🚩 Self-check: are you under-fueling?

  • Periods lighter or stopped
  • Always tired, irritable, sleeping poorly
  • Frequent little injuries / getting sick often
  • Strength stalling or dropping, easy fractures

Two or more? Take it seriously—fix "eat enough" first; a stopped period always warrants a doctor.

🚫 Don't

  • Chronically train fasted in the morning
  • Stack intermittent fasting on top of high training volume
  • Slash carbs very low to lose fat—your hormones pay first

4Perimenopause / menopause—lift heavier, not lighter

As estrogen falls, muscle is harder to build, bone is lost faster, fat gathers around the middle, and recovery slows. Many women switch to "more walking, more yoga, less lifting" at exactly this point—the opposite of what helps. This stage calls for a stronger stimulus.

Evidence: resistance + protein + creatine solid; exact sprint/jump dosing is more inference

✅ Do this (sample week)

  • 2 heavy strength sessions: compound-focused (squat/deadlift/bench/row/press), 3–4 sets × 5–8 reps, a load that's hard by the last 1–2 reps.
  • 1 sprint-interval session: run or bike sprints 20–30 s × 6–8, full rest between—more bang than long slow cardio.
  • Bone-loading jumps: after lifting, add 2–3 sets of jumps/landings (pogo jumps, box jumps, skipping)—only if knees/ankles are healthy.
  • Push protein to the top end: 1.6–2.2 g/kg; creatine 3–5 g daily (any time, no "loading phase"—benefits women's muscle and brain, and is safe).
  • Sleep first: don't let training pile into chronic fatigue; cut aimless long slow cardio.
On hormone therapy (MHT): whether to use it is a separate conversation with your doctor—it doesn't conflict with "lift heavy," they run in parallel.

🚀 Do these 5 this week

  1. Swap one "just move a bit" session for a real heavy strength workout.
  2. Eat something before training—stop grinding fasted.
  3. From today, hit ~30 g protein at every meal.
  4. Note one line a day: energy/sleep/training + which cycle day.
  5. (Perimenopause) Add 3–5 g creatine daily + one sprint-interval session.
